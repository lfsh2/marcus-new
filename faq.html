Is this just another accountability program?
No — and that distinction matters. Accountability programs are built on the premise that if you confess your sin to another man regularly, you'll stop doing it. You've already tried that. It's not that the men weren't good men or the programs were bad programs. It's that accountability addresses the surface behavior, not the root wound. The Sonship Path goes after the father wound and the theology underneath the behavior. That's a fundamentally different thing.
How is this different from therapy?
Most therapists — even excellent ones — are not specifically trained in the theology of sonship or the father wound as it relates to sexual brokenness in Christian men. I've had clients who did years of high-quality therapy and still needed something different to break the pattern. I work in the intersection of grace-based theology, IFS (Internal Family Systems), nervous system regulation, and inner healing prayer. That's a specific lane most therapists don't occupy. Many of my clients continue with a therapist alongside our work — that's actually encouraged.
I've tried Every Man's Battle, Covenant Eyes, Celebrate Recovery. Why would this work?
Because those programs are all built on the same theological foundation: identify your sin, confess it, resist it harder, stay accountable to someone who will call you out if you fail. That foundation is shame-based — and shame, neuroscientifically, activates the same nervous system state that drives men back to the behavior in the first place. Shame produces more of what you're trying to escape. The Sonship Path is built on the exact opposite foundation: you are a beloved son, not a condemned sinner trying to get clean. Grace — not stricter accountability — is what produces lasting behavioral change. I've lived this. That's not a theological idea to me, it's what actually broke a 26-year cycle.
Is this only for Black men?
No. My voice and story naturally resonate most with Black men from evangelical or Pentecostal backgrounds — that is who I am and I don't apologize for the specificity. But The Sonship Path is built on universal truths about the father wound, sonship theology, and nervous system healing that apply to any man, from any background. If the approach speaks to where you are, you belong here.
I'm still in ministry / corporate leadership. Can I do this while staying in my role?
That's exactly who I built this for — and exactly who I am. I built and run The Sonship Path while actively serving as a Senior Director of Finance and raising three kids. My clients are senior pastors, corporate attorneys, executives, high school teachers, and ministry leaders. I understand the specific pressure of carrying something like this in a high-visibility role. The work doesn't require you to go public, blow up your life, or step down from anything. It requires you to be honest with me and willing to go where the work leads.
My wife doesn't know. Do I have to tell her before we start?
No, it's not a requirement before we begin. Several men I've worked with came in without their wives knowing — and chose the right moment to tell them partway through coaching, after they'd built enough stability in themselves to hold the conversation well. We'll work through the disclosure question together as part of the container if and when it becomes relevant. The goal is always a conversation that serves the marriage, not one that blows it up prematurely.
How long until I see real results?
Most men report genuine shifts — in how their nervous system responds to triggers, in their felt sense of the Father's love, and in the frequency and intensity of compulsive patterns — within the first 6 to 8 sessions. Lasting, integrated freedom that holds up under real-life pressure typically takes the full 6-month arc. I won't tell you this is a quick fix — it isn't. But it is actual healing, and actual healing is permanent in a way that behavioral management never is. The men I've walked through report that the freedom continues to deepen even after the formal coaching ends.
What if I relapse while we're working together?
A relapse is information — not a failure, and certainly not a disqualifier. If you slip during our work together, we bring it into the next session and use it. What did the slip reveal about what hasn't been healed yet? What emotional state or memory was present? What did the behavior offer you in that moment that you couldn't access any other way? I am never going to shame you for a relapse. I'm going to help you understand what it's telling us. Almost every man I've worked with sees the frequency and intensity of their patterns significantly reduce within the first few months — even before they experience what they'd call "freedom." That reduction is itself evidence that the root work is happening.
Is this biblically sound? I'm concerned about inner healing prayer and IFS.
That's a fair and important question. Yes — everything I do is theologically grounded. Inner healing prayer is encounter-based ministry rooted in the biblical reality that Jesus is present in our memories and can redeem them (Psalm 139, Hebrews 13:8). IFS (Internal Family Systems) is a psychological model I use as a framework for understanding the different "parts" of a person that scripture acknowledges — a model completely compatible with a biblical anthropology. I am not a therapist making spiritual claims, nor a minister making therapeutic claims. I'm a coach who integrates both — and I hold both to the same standard of truth. If anything feels off to you theologically, bring it to the call and I'll explain my thinking directly.
